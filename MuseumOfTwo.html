The Museum Of Two [MOT] is situated at the border of two districts: a recent residential neighborhood and a former industrial area which the city of Haarlem is planning to transform. The municipality wants the district to become mixed in use whereas the industry moves further east. However, the area lacks of public space due to its‘ in- dustrial history. While the residential neighborhood was developed with some semi-private squares for the direct neighbors, a mixed district needs facilities for a wider public.
As a pilot project for the new usage, the MOT is designed as a park with small pavilions. Their shape is formed by the potential movement of people entering the plot. The curves create a pulling effect on the park for neighbors and visitors.
The pavilions are connected underground to maintain the open park. This basement contains the two exhibition spaces which are connected to the environment with sky- lights through the pavilion volumes. The exhibition itself has a focus on Dutch sculptors of the 20th century and contemporary artists that are invited to live and work wit- hin the museum. After selecting and placing the artworks, the curves were redefined and set to their final form.

Two brickwork buildings of a former power station are situated on the plot. Whereas one of the buildings is in good condition and therefore integrated into the design, the other one is falling apart. Several other ruins surround the plot. Considering these resources I researched brick reuse and on the methodology of Gamle Mursten; a company that focuses on brick reuse. The brickwork can be demolished with common methods but has to be dispatched quickly to avoid damages from the const- ruction site. For further processing, the brick is cleaned mechanically and tested commonly on stability. In that way, the reused brick saves 95% of manufacturing energy. Therefore, in Combination with the other resources from surrounding ruins, the materialization of the MOT consists mainly of brick and glass.
